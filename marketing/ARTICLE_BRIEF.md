## Article Specifications

### a) Length Target
**1,200-1,800 words** (approximately 6-9 minute read)

**Why this length?**
- Long enough to tell the full story with emotional depth
- Short enough that busy people on Reddit/HN will finish it
- Optimal for Medium's algorithm (7-minute reads perform best)
- Allows for narrative arc: problem → struggle → insight → solution → results

**Structure breakdown:**
- Hook/Opening: 100-150 words
- Problem section: 300-400 words
- Insight/Aha moment: 200-300 words
- Solution description: 400-500 words
- Results/Impact: 200-300 words
- Closing/Call-to-action: 100-150 words

---

### b) Target Audience

**Primary Reader:** "Past You" - someone currently experiencing what you experienced

**Demographic:**
- Age: 28-45 years old
- Profession: Knowledge workers, developers, designers, entrepreneurs
- Tech-savvy: Uses productivity apps, reads Hacker News or r/productivity
- Pain level: High - has tried 3+ task managers and failed with all of them

**Psychographic:**
- Feels guilt about their growing task list
- Intellectually knows they should "let go" but can't emotionally
- Tired of productivity advice that adds more pressure
- Seeks authenticity over hacks/tricks
- Values thoughtfulness over speed

**What they're thinking when they find this article:**
- "I can't be the only one who feels overwhelmed by task managers"
- "Why do I keep abandoning these apps?"
- "There must be a better way than endless organizing"

**Where they'll read it:**
- Reddit during lunch break (phone or desktop)
- Hacker News on Sunday morning (laptop with coffee)
- Medium recommended articles (tablet in bed)
- Shared link from a friend who said "this is so you"

**What they need from this article:**
1. **Validation:** "Someone else gets it - I'm not broken"
2. **Understanding:** "Here's WHY traditional task managers fail for people like us"
3. **Hope:** "There's actually a solution that works differently"
4. **Clarity:** "I understand what this does and why it would help me"
5. **Low-friction next step:** "I can try this without committing to yet another system"

### c) Dos and Don'ts

#### DO ✅

**Emotional Honesty:**
- Share the specific moment you felt defeated by a task manager
- Describe the guilt of seeing 247 unfinished tasks
- Admit you felt "broken" for not being able to use the Eisenhower Matrix
- Use vulnerable language: "I couldn't..." not "Users often struggle to..."

**Concrete Details:**
- Name the specific apps you tried (Things 3, Remember the Milk)
- Give real numbers (30% of tasks in graveyard, 30 days threshold)
- Describe the physical notebook-tearing moment in sensory detail
- Show, don't just tell: "I'd open Things 3, see 247 tasks, and close it immediately"

**Reader-Centric Language:**
- Use "you" and "your" frequently
- Ask questions: "Does this sound familiar?"
- Make it conversational, like talking to a friend
- Assume they've experienced exactly what you have

**Build Credibility:**
- Mention you're a developer/builder (establishes technical credibility)
- Show you understand the problem deeply (not just selling something)
- Acknowledge what you tried before building (shows research/humility)
- Be specific about the inspiration (notebook-tearing story)

**Create Intrigue:**
- Lead with the counterintuitive hook: "deletes your tasks"
- Use the word "Graveyard" - it's memorable and slightly provocative
- Reveal the 30% statistic strategically (not in the first paragraph)
- End sections with open loops that pull readers forward

**Provide Value Even If They Don't Download:**
- The insight about "ignored for 30 days = not important" is useful alone
- Explain why Eisenhower Matrix fails for certain personalities
- Readers should feel they learned something even if they don't try the app

#### DON'T ❌

**Avoid Salesy Language:**
- Never say "amazing," "revolutionary," "game-changing"
- Don't list features like a spec sheet
- Don't use superlatives: "the best," "the only," "the ultimate"
- No pressure tactics: "limited time," "join thousands," "what are you waiting for?"

**Don't Oversell:**
- Don't claim it works for everyone (it won't)
- Don't compare yourself to competitors by name in a negative way
- Don't promise outcomes you can't guarantee
- Don't use fake urgency or scarcity

**Avoid Generic Productivity Clichés:**
- Don't start with "In today's fast-paced world..."
- Skip the "time is our most valuable resource" platitudes
- No quotes from famous people about productivity
- Avoid the word "hack" entirely

**Don't Lose the Thread:**
- Don't go on tangents about other productivity topics
- Don't explain every feature of the app (save for documentation)
- Don't turn this into a tutorial (just tell the story)
- Don't break the narrative flow with bullet points or lists (until the solution section)

**Don't Hide Your Investment:**
- Don't pretend to be a neutral observer
- Don't write in third person about "the app"
- Don't be coy about the fact that you built it
- But also don't make it about "look what I built" - keep focus on solving the problem

**Avoid Technical Jargon:**
- Don't mention SwiftUI, SwiftData, CloudKit, etc.
- Skip technical architecture discussions
- Don't explain how the Graveyard is implemented
- Speak in user outcomes, not technical features

**Don't Preach:**
- Don't tell people they're using task managers "wrong"
- Don't be condescending about other approaches
- Don't make people feel bad for their current struggles
- Stay in "I" language, not "you should" language

### d) Suggested Style

**Tone:** Reflective, honest, slightly contrarian, ultimately hopeful

**Think:**
- Paul Graham essay (intellectual but accessible)
- Derek Sivers post (concise, personal, insightful)
- Tim Urban (Wait But Why) without the stick figures (deep but conversational)

**Voice Characteristics:**

**1. Conversational, not corporate**
- "I tried Things 3" not "I evaluated several task management solutions"
- "I'd open the app and immediately close it" not "User engagement decreased over time"
- Write like you're explaining this to a smart friend over coffee

**2. Self-aware, not self-deprecating**
- "I couldn't let go of tasks - everything felt important" (honest vulnerability)
- NOT "I'm terrible at productivity" (undermines credibility)
- Own your struggle without making yourself the punchline

**3. Insightful, not instructive**
- "Here's what I realized..." not "Here's what you should do..."
- Share discoveries rather than giving commands
- Let readers come to their own conclusions

**4. Specific, not abstract**
- "30 days of ignoring a task" not "an extended period of inactivity"
- "247 tasks" not "an overwhelming number of items"
- "Tearing out notebook pages" not "removing completed items from a physical system"

**5. Story-driven, not feature-driven**
- Organize around emotional journey, not product capabilities
- The Graveyard is a character in your story, not a feature bullet point
- Lead with narrative, support with details

**Sentence Structure:**
- Vary length: mix short punchy sentences with longer flowing ones
- Use short paragraphs (2-4 sentences max for web reading)
- Occasional one-sentence paragraphs for emphasis
- Start some sentences with "And" or "But" for rhythm (this is a blog, not formal writing)

**Example of the style:**

> I tried Things 3 for six months. Every morning, I'd review my tasks. Every evening, I'd add new ones. And every week, the list grew longer.
>
> The advice was always the same: organize better. Add tags. Create projects. Use the Eisenhower Matrix to identify what's truly important.
>
> But here's the thing: I couldn't mark anything as "not important." Everything on my list felt like it mattered. The article I wanted to write, the feature I wanted to build, the book I wanted to read - they all felt urgent, important, both.
>
> So the list kept growing. Until I stopped opening the app altogether.

**Emotional Arc:**
1. **Opening:** Frustration (relatable pain)
2. **Problem:** Recognition (oh, I'm not alone)
3. **Struggle:** Resignation (I tried everything)
4. **Insight:** Curiosity (wait, what if...)
5. **Solution:** Understanding (that makes sense)
6. **Results:** Hope (maybe this could work for me too)

### e) What Else You Should Know/Challenge Before Starting

#### Pre-Writing Questions to Answer

**1. What's the ONE thing you want readers to remember?**
- If they forget everything else, what's the core insight?
- Suggested answer: "If you ignore a task for 30 days, it wasn't actually important"
- Challenge: Can you make this insight hit harder than it currently does?

**2. What's the notebook-tearing story in full detail?**
- Where were you when you saw this?
- Who was the person doing it?
- What exactly did they say about why they were doing it?
- How did you feel watching it?
- **This is your article's centerpiece - flesh it out fully before writing**

**3. What was the specific moment you abandoned Things 3 (or your last app)?**
- What triggered it?
- How many tasks did you have?
- What was the task that made you think "I can't do this anymore"?
- This should be a vivid scene, not a vague memory

**4. What percentage of tasks are actually important?**
- You said 30% go to graveyard
- That means 70% stay active
- But of that 70%, how many become priorities?
- Can you make a case that only 10% of tasks truly matter?

**5. What will skeptics say?**
- "But what if I delete something important?"
- "This is just encouraging laziness"
- "I need to keep tasks for reference"
- **Pre-address these in the article without making it defensive**

#### Strategic Challenges

**Challenge #1: The Hook**
"I Built a Task Manager That Deletes Your Tasks" is good, but test alternatives:
- "I Solved My Task Overwhelm by Deleting 30% of My Tasks Automatically"
- "The Task Manager That Tears Out Pages for You"
- "Why I Built a Task Manager That Fights Your Productivity"

Which one makes YOU want to keep reading? That's your title.

**Challenge #2: The Insight**
The notebook-tearing moment is powerful. But is there a deeper insight?
- Why do we keep tasks we'll never do?
- What does our task list really represent? (anxiety? ambition? identity?)
- Is the problem the tasks themselves, or our relationship with them?

Don't answer these in the article necessarily - but thinking through them will deepen your writing.

**Challenge #3: The Ending**
Most blog posts fail at the ending. They either:
- Fizzle out ("so yeah, check it out")
- Get salesy ("Download now!")
- Repeat what they already said

Your ending should:
- Give the reader permission to let go
- Provide a small, concrete next step (even if not downloading)
- Leave them thinking about their own task list differently

**Suggested ending approach:**
> "Right now, you probably have a task list somewhere. Maybe it's in Things 3, maybe it's in a notebook, maybe it's scattered across sticky notes and browser tabs.
>
> Here's what I want you to do: Open it. Scroll to the bottom. Find the tasks you've been ignoring for weeks.
>
> They're not important. I know you think they are. But if they were, you'd have done them.
>
> You don't need my app to figure that out. You just need permission to let them go.
>
> (But if you want something that does the letting go for you, I built that too. It's called Three Daily Goals. About 30% of my tasks quietly move to the graveyard — and honestly, I've never missed one. It frees up so much mental space.)"

#### Writing Process Recommendations

**1. Write the middle first**
- Start with the notebook-tearing scene
- That's your anchor - everything else builds around it
- Don't worry about the intro until you know where you're going

**2. Use the "So what?" test**
- After every paragraph, ask: "So what?"
- If you can't answer why the reader cares, cut it or rewrite it

**3. Read it out loud**
- If you can't speak it naturally, rewrite it
- Awkward sentences reveal themselves when spoken

**4. Cut 20% after your first draft**
- First drafts are always too long
- Every sentence should earn its place
- If something doesn't advance the story or insight, delete it

**5. Test the hook on someone**
- Show the first 3 paragraphs to a friend
- Ask: "Would you keep reading?"
- If they hesitate, rewrite the opening

#### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

**Pitfall #1: Making it about the app too soon**
- Don't mention the app name until at least 50% through
- Build the problem and insight first
- The app is the answer to a question readers need to be asking

**Pitfall #2: Explaining too much**
- You don't need to explain every feature
- You don't need to justify every design decision
- Trust that the core insight is enough

**Pitfall #3: Hedging your language**
- Don't say "kind of," "sort of," "maybe," "I think"
- Commit to your points
- "This works" not "This might work for some people"

**Pitfall #4: Writing to impress instead of connect**
- No one cares about your vocabulary
- They care about whether you understand their problem
- Simple, clear language wins

**Pitfall #5: Forgetting the reader halfway through**
- It's easy to drift into talking about yourself
- Keep asking: "Why does the reader care about this sentence?"
- Bring it back to their experience

#### Success Criteria

**You'll know the article works if:**
1. Someone who has never used a task manager understands the problem
2. Someone who loves Things 3 feels genuinely curious about trying something different
3. Someone who has given up on task managers feels hope
4. The article stands alone as valuable even without downloading the app
5. You feel slightly vulnerable hitting "publish" (means you were honest)

#### The Meta-Question

**Why are you writing this article?**

Is it to:
- Get downloads? (marketing goal)
- Share an insight that helped you? (value goal)
- Find people who think like you? (community goal)
- Establish credibility in the productivity space? (positioning goal)

The answer should be: **All of the above, in that order.**

But if you had to pick ONE, which would it be? That will guide your tone.

My recommendation: Write it primarily to **share the insight that helped you**. If you do that authentically, the downloads will follow. People can smell when you're writing to sell vs. writing to help.

## Outline Framework (Use This as Your Starting Point)

### Working Title Options
1. "I Built an Open-Source Task Manager That Deletes Your Tasks (And It Saved My Productivity)"
2. "Why I Built - And Open Sourced - A Task Manager That Tears Out Pages for You"
3. "The Open-Source Task Manager That Finally Let Me Let Go"
4. "I Built a Task Manager That Deletes Your Tasks - Here's Why I Open Sourced It"

### Section 1: The Hook (100-150 words)
- Open with the counterintuitive promise: deleting tasks
- Establish relatability: you've tried everything
- Promise: here's what actually worked

### Section 2: The Problem (300-400 words)
- Your history with task managers (Things 3, Remember the Milk, etc.)
- The pattern: excitement → accumulation → guilt → abandonment
- The Eisenhower Matrix failure: "I couldn't mark anything as unimportant"
- The emotional weight: what it feels like to see 247 tasks

### Section 3: The Insight (200-300 words)
- **THE NOTEBOOK SCENE** - describe it fully
- The realization: ignoring = proof of unimportance
- Why this is different from willpower or decision-making
- The "aha" moment: what if this happened automatically?

### Section 4: The Solution (400-500 words)
- What you built: automatic task graveyard (30 days of no activity)
- How it works in practice: silent, automatic, no guilt
- The results: 30% of tasks go there, zero regrets
- The other pieces: 3 daily goals, energy-matching, 5-minute ritual
- Why this works when other things didn't
- **Why you open sourced it:** Aligns with "letting go" philosophy - no lock-in, transparent, prioritizes impact over control

### Section 5: The Impact (200-300 words)
- What changed for you personally
- The emotional shift: relief instead of pressure
- The practical shift: finishing things vs. organizing things
- Acknowledging this won't work for everyone (and that's okay)

### Section 6: The Invitation (100-150 words)
- Give readers permission to let go (they can do this without your app)
- Offer the app as a tool for those who want automation
- **Mention it's open source:** "If you want to see how it works, the code is on GitHub. If you just want to use it, it's on the App Store."
- Soft call-to-action: no pressure, just availability - support it by buying, starring on GitHub, or sharing
- End with hope: your list doesn't have to own you
