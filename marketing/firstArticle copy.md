# I Built an Open-Source Task Manager that kills your tasks for you

I found out that task management is not about ticking boxes and finishing tasks. I learned that a good task manager is helping you to separate important from not important tasks. 

But how do you distinguish between important and distraction? I cannot separate myself from all the "great" ideas that I produce. How could I [kill my own "puppy"](https://analytical-mind.com/2009/12/01/would-you-have-the-courage-to-kill-your-puppy/)? And what does it have to do with Buckets vs Rivers? 



## What did I try before I built a task manager that kills your tasks for you
I tried several task managers. First time, it was Remember the Milk. I came across this little tool by accident during my studies and was in love with its cool name, the maskot, the simplicity. Well, actually, you can tweek it quite a bit. I optimised it. And I added tasks at a rate higher than I could tick them off. And then it happened. I couldn't stand the ever growing list of tasks. I felt defeated. 

Years later, I tried it again. This time I did some research. I choose the very well reviewed, very polished and really good task manager Things. I can highly recommend it. But I didn't understand my problem and hence I was up for a 2nd disappointment, this time "with style". 

Still not understanding, I tried again after a year or two a different kind of task manager: an Eisenhower Matrix based. But I realised that I am not able to say "not important". If I would say the idea is not important, I should kill it immediately. Why should I waste my limited time on something unimportant? However, I like my ideas and they all seem important. How could I kill any of my "fantastic" ideas? I am simply not in the business of killing ideas (or puppies).  So, I basically only used urgent/non-urgent differentiation as all ideas were important. And again, tasks were coming faster than being removed as hardly any task was deemed not important - because that means I can ignore them. 

My problem: I know I need a task manager, but I cannot handle it. I am stockpiling tasks and feel bad about it until the point where I stop using the very tool I need. I tried to compensate by sending myself text messages, etc. But it is not a good situation, too messy, too unstructured, too unreliable. 


## Why I built a task manager that kills your tasks for you
How do others cope with run away task lists? 
How do they separate them enough from their ideas that they can say "this is actually not important"? 
Surely, I know I need to manage my tasks but I don't want to try a 4th task manager and feel like failing myself again. 
I cannot be the only one. 

And so I read through online community pages, trying to find an answer. 
There are endless discussons about which task manager is the best. 
But nobody seem to be writing about why they fail using Task Managers. 
I find a discussion about why one would even use task managers, one could just use a sheets of paper. 
Well, that is way too unstructured for me. 
And I won't have always the piece of paper with me. 

And then, I struck gold. 
Someone is describing his process, based on something called bullet journalling. 
He has a book that he does carry around. 
Tasks are getting added at the bottom, line by line. 
Every day he goes through the pages, oldest to new and strikes through tasks completed and copies a few tasks to his daily list. 
Ok, nice. 
But then he writes something like "and after a while I tear out pages with the old tasks." 
He found out that if a task wasn't done for quite a while that he couldn't deem it important enough to work on, then these tasks simply aren't important and can be thrown away. 

Now, that is new to me. 
And it is "bold": throwing away tasks just because you didn't act on them - this is genius!

## How this task manager is killing tasks for you
From this moment, I put my main project to a stop and focused on a task manager that translates this tearing away of ignored tasks into a digital solution that I can use on my iPhone and on my Mac. How? 

How could it work? 
What is the digital equivalent of tearing pages? 
Would it even work digitally? 

I experimented with the idea. 
Deleting the task was too radical for me - you remember, I like my ideas. 

I came up with a simple rule: "a task that wasn't touched for 30 days is obviously not important and therefore is moved to the (digital) graveyard". 
To make it work, I created a daily process (dubbed: "Compass Check"). 
Every day (actually from noon to noon) I plan a day in advance, taking stress and surprises out of my life. 
As part of the daily Compass Check I go through the list of open tasks and select a few as priority for that day, remembering the "Three Daily Goals" of [J.D. Meier](https://sourcesofinsight.com/day-3-daily-outcomes-use-three-stories-to-drive-your-day/), or more generally the [Rule of Three](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_three).

These priority tasks are then displayed promintly in the app and via iOS and macOS widgets. 
The Compass Check is fully configurable and has several steps, including a step that "kills" tasks after 30 days. 
Another step is a categorisation inta an Energy-Effort Matrix to get a feel for the task.
And during the Plan the Day step is the task manager creating calendar entries for your tasks. 
This is done because tasks are more likely getting done when you plan when to do them (see [Implementation Intentions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implementation_intention)). [^1]

And if you like gamification: I included a streak counter for every consequtive day of running a compass check. 

I use my task manager to collect task from everywhere via sharing extensions, both on iOS and macOS. 
About 30% of my tasks quietly move to the graveyard — and I've never missed one. That alone frees up so much mental space.
I can always review what I have in the graveyard, including notes and attachments.
Sometimes, I move a task back into open, but mostly I've learned to let go on my own too. 

## How killing tasks is changing my life

You ask what is the impact of this task manager? 
I am right now on the longest streak to use a task manager I ever had. 
I am never overwhelmed by the number of tasks. 

A simple review of the day to come can take a lot of stress out of your life. 
How many times was I surprised when a meeting reminder popped up on my computer.  
And with the widgets as a reminder of the daily priorities and the implementation intentions I am doing more of my plans. 

Another change is more subtle, 
If you accept that not every task needs to be done, you take stress out of your life. 
I like Oliver Burkeman's [Rivers and Buckets](https://www.oliverburkeman.com/river) (or see this nice [Sketchplanations](https://sketchplanations.com/rivers-and-buckets)) and it helps me navigating throught this crazy world as best as I can. 
I can do a lot. 
But I cannot do everything that I would like to do.
